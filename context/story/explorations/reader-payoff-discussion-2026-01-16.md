## Background

The PR proposes an anti-genre approach where the investigator is *not* transformed by discovering cosmic impossibilities. They just close the file and go home.

Jon's initial questions (from PR comments):
> "What's the payoff for the reader? What inspires them to recommend the story to a friend?"

The existing brainstorm proposed:
- Cumulative payoff through the testimonies themselves
- Reader disturbed by investigator's unnatural calm
- Knowing withholding as deliberate choice

## Riffing / Ideas

### On the vignette approach

> "I'd like to express a strong agreement with the vignette approach. Love it. Let each one be a self-contained story that has a beginning, middle, and end. Is that possible in the current format? If not, can we do anything to get there?"

### On the investigator's lack of curiosity

> "I chuckled at the 'gets hit by a bus' approach. If there are 12 self-contained stories that show slices of humanity, and then he just gets hit by a bus, that's funny."

But the stronger idea:

> "I like using this as a way to communicate that some people are curious and some aren't."

Possible executions:
- Talking to his wife—she asks for details, he says "not much to say"
- He's killed, and at the funeral they mention he was "never quite curious"
- Maybe he's only curious about *other* things (not the cosmic/human stuff)

The core insight:

> "Some way of referring to how some people can see humanity and interest in anything at all... and others wouldn't know a thing if it bit them on the nose. That'd be great. If the 12 stories are amazing and the message is delivered that it's wasted on him, that's cool."

---

## Clarifying Questions

### Q1: Vignette format—testimonies vs. stories?

> "I don't know. I don't know how to make testimonies pack enough of a punch. I want them to feel lived in and human. A flat testimony where a weird thing happens, followed by a shrug ending doesn't feel rewarding."

**Implication**: We need to solve this. The testimony format may need rethinking—or the frame around them needs to carry emotional weight.

### Q2: Curiosity as trait vs. flaw?

> "Fine to critique I think. A parable about AI. Some people don't even see the world shifting on them."

**Implication**: This is explicitly a critique. The investigator represents people who miss transformation happening around them. Resonates with AI moment—some people see it, some don't.

### Q3: Who's the curious one?

> "Both. You get frustrated and then the reader is brought into the scene, perhaps?"

**Implication**: Reader frustration first, then a character arrives who voices/embodies that frustration.

### Q4: Explicit vs. implied?

> "Implied. Show don't tell."

### Q5: The landing?

> "No idea, help me imagine."

## 10 Ideas for the Curious Character

*Seeded from plots of 90s alt-rock songs for divergent texture*

### 1. The Creep (Radiohead)
The investigator's *intern* is obsessed with the case. Feels like a weirdo for caring. Watches him close the file and can't believe it. After he leaves, they photocopy the testimonies. They don't belong here—but they're the only one who sees.

### 2. The Teen Spirit (Nirvana)
A *journalist* covering the bureaucratic merger stumbles on the story. Nihilistic at first—"whatever, just another government shuffle." But something in testimony #7 hooks them. They try to interview the investigator. He says: "It's just paperwork." They realize the apathy is the horror.

### 3. The Religion (R.E.M.)
The investigator's *wife* was always the curious one. She's been asking about his work for 30 years. This time she pushes harder. He deflects. She goes quiet. In the corner of her eye, in the spotlight—she realizes she married someone who will never see what she sees.

### 4. The Black Hole Sun (Soundgarden)
One of the *testimony-givers* is still alive. An old woman in a care home. A curious grad student tracks her down for their thesis. She describes the impossible hallway. Beautiful, wrong. The student records everything. The investigator never called her.

### 5. The Bitter Sweet Symphony (The Verve)
The investigator *knows* something is strange. He's not stupid. But he's three years from pension. He's a slave to the routine. The curious part of him died decades ago. We see a flashback: young him, asking questions. The system ground it out of him. He chose comfort.

### 6. The Karma Police (Radiohead)
A *whistleblower* inside the agency has been waiting for someone to notice. They feed the investigator hints. He ignores them. They escalate. He reports them to HR. In the end, they're the ones consumed—fired, discredited. He files that paperwork too.

### 7. The 1979 (Smashing Pumpkins)
The awkward acquaintance aboard the Kepler? It was someone from his *hometown*. A kid he barely knew. Flashback to 1979: summer, driving around, a moment that felt important. The kid asked him once: "Don't you ever wonder what's out there?" He said no. Forty years later, still no.

### 8. The No Rain (Blind Melon)
A testimony describes a *child* aboard the station who kept asking questions—"Why is the hallway different? Why do the clocks disagree?" The adults shushed her. She found one person who listened: the station librarian. The child's curiosity is the heart of testimony #9. The investigator skims it.

### 9. The Wonderwall (Oasis)
The investigator's *daughter* calls him that night. She's going through something. She needs him to be curious about her life. He gives his usual responses. After he hangs up, she sits in silence. The testimonies would have taught him how to listen—if he'd read them.

### 10. The Everlong (Foo Fighters)
One testimony describes two people who *fell in love* during the Kepler incident. In the impossible moment—when time fractured—they held onto each other. They describe wanting to stay there forever. The investigator notes the inconsistency in their timestamps and moves on.

## Ending Options

**A. The Non-Ending**: Files it. Dinner. TV. Bed. Horror is the mundanity.

**B. The Almost**: A phrase in testimony #12 makes him pause. Almost looks back. Doesn't.

**C. The Transfer**: Done, but we follow the file to someone else's desk. New hands. Eyes widen. Cut to black.

**D. The Echo**: Years later. Retired or dead. Someone finds his notes. A single margin scribble: "Doesn't add up." He *noticed*. Just didn't care.

**E. The Bus**: Walks out, gets hit by a bus. Testimonies scatter. Stranger picks one up. Face changes.

**F. The Mirror**: Final scene is wife/daughter/intern reading the testimonies we just read. Same words. Different reaction.

**Note**: Ending doesn't need to be decided yet. Let the 12 testimonies guide what feels right.

## Alignment Analysis

*Checked against `taste.md` and `ideal-state.md`*

### Jon's Key Preferences (from taste.md)
- Testimonies should feel like real people with real lives
- Show how institutions actually work—incentives, constraints, information flow
- Competence matters more than cynicism
- **Biggest pet peeve**: Lazy shorthand where government is dumb, corporations corrupt, only lone hackers see truth

### Strongest Alignments

| Idea | Why It Aligns |
|------|---------------|
| **#5 Bitter Sweet Symphony** | Shows how the system grinds down curiosity without making it cartoonishly evil. He's not stupid—he *chose* comfort. Three years to pension. That's a real constraint, not cynicism. |
| **#9 Wonderwall (Daughter)** | Personal stakes, not heroic. The testimonies could have taught him to listen—to her. Connects cosmic themes to domestic reality. |
| **#3 Religion (Wife)** | 30 years of marriage as constraint. She realizes something about him. Not an outsider-hero; just someone who lives with him and finally sees. |
| **#7 The 1979 (Hometown)** | Establishes the pattern over a lifetime. He was always like this. The kid asked "Don't you wonder?" and he said no. Forty years later, still no. Earned, not arbitrary. |

### Strong for Testimony-as-Payoff Structure

| Idea | Why It Works |
|------|--------------|
| **#8 No Rain (Child)** | The child's curiosity IS testimony #9. Curiosity lives in the data, not in an outside hero. |
| **#10 Everlong (Love story)** | Two people held each other while time fractured. He notes the timestamp inconsistency and moves on. Humanity vs. bureaucracy in one beat. |

### Riskier (Watch for Pet Peeve)

| Idea | Risk |
|------|------|
| **#1 Creep (Intern)** | Risks "lone outsider sees truth" pattern. Could work if intern doesn't become hero. |
| **#2 Teen Spirit (Journalist)** | Journalist-as-truth-seeker is overused. |
| **#4 Black Hole Sun (Grad Student)** | Another outsider-sees-truth. Works better as testimony content than frame. |
| **#6 Karma Police (Whistleblower)** | Good institutional mechanics but darkest option. Risks cynicism if mishandled. |

### Recommendation

**Combine:**
- **#5 (Bitter Sweet)** as investigator backstory—he wasn't always like this, the system made him this way
- **#9 (Wonderwall)** or **#3 (Religion)** as frame contrast—personal cost shown through someone who lives with him
- **#8 (No Rain)** and **#10 (Everlong)** as testimony content—curiosity and humanity live IN the testimonies themselves

This gives: System critique without cynicism + Personal stakes without heroism + Testimonies carry the emotional weight

### For Ending

Most aligned with taste: **B (The Almost)** or **D (The Echo)**—both acknowledge he's not stupid, just chose not to engage.

Most aligned with Douglas Adams energy Jon loves: **E (The Bus)**—absurdist, doesn't overexplain, lets testimonies scatter to someone who might care.
